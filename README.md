# Financial-crisis-predictions
Financial crises have repeatedly disrupted global economies, causing severe economic instability, unemployment, and financial losses. Events such as the Asian Financial Crisis (1997), the Global Financial Crisis (2008), and more recent economic downturns have demonstrated how rapidly financial instability can spread across countries and financial systems. These crises often originate from imbalances in macroeconomic indicators such as declining foreign reserves, rapid currency depreciation, high debt levels, or unstable banking systems. Detecting these warning signals at an early stage is essential for governments, financial institutions, and policymakers to implement preventive measures and minimize economic damage. Traditional economic monitoring methods often rely on manual analysis and statistical models that may fail to capture complex relationships between multiple economic variables. With the increasing availability of large-scale economic data, modern data-driven approaches have become valuable tools for analyzing financial stability. Machine learning techniques, in particular, provide the ability to analyze complex patterns within large datasets and identify hidden relationships that may indicate upcoming financial instability. Traditional early warning systems (Kaminsky–Lizondo–Reinhart, Frankel–Rose) rely on static thresholds or econometric models that struggle with nonlinear relationships. Recent machine learning approaches (Random Forest, XGBoost) have improved predictive performance, but most studies focus on either a single country group or a fixed prediction horizon (typically 12–24 months).
